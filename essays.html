Experience design · 2025

The designed situation

People do not change when they understand something new. They change when their behaviour becomes impossible to continue. The situation is where that impossibility lives.

There is a persistent belief in organisational life that the right content, delivered well, produces change. A workshop with good material. A facilitator who explains clearly. Slides that are both honest and elegant. I used to believe this too. I have a shelf of evidence that it is not quite right.

What I have come to notice is this: people already know most of what they need to know. A leadership team in difficulty is rarely short of information about the difficulty. They know they are avoiding something. They know the conversation has not happened. They know the strategy exists only on paper. What they do not have is a situation in which doing something different is more available than doing the same thing.

You cannot teach someone to lead under pressure.
You can design a situation where leadership either emerges — or doesn't.

The designed situation does not explain. It does not instruct. It places people in contact with each other and with a set of conditions they did not fully choose — and then waits. What emerges is not performance. It is behaviour. And behaviour, once visible, is workable in a way that understanding alone never is.

The designer's task is surprisingly narrow. Create the conditions. Remove the exits. Be present without intervening unnecessarily. Trust the room to do what rooms do when the situation is right.

Leadership · 2025

After the workshop

Three days after the workshop, something has already been forgotten. Three weeks after, the notebook is in a drawer. Three months after, the organisation is more or less where it was. This is not a failure of motivation. It is a failure of design.

The question I find most useful when someone asks me to design a development programme is not: what do people need to learn? It is: what would have to happen for anything to be different in six months?

These are not the same question. The first points toward content. The second points toward conditions — the actual conditions of the actual organisation, with its actual power dynamics, its particular history, its specific capacity for discomfort.

The body remembers what the mind forgets. What people carry from an experience is almost never the content of a slide. It is the feeling of a room at a specific moment. The silence before someone said the thing no one had been saying. The moment when something became impossible to ignore. These are the experiences that create durable change — not because they were instructive, but because they were real.

Design for the moment that cannot be forgotten.
Everything else is furniture.

This means accepting that the designer does not control the outcome. You can create conditions for a significant moment. You cannot guarantee one. What you can do is make the significant moment more likely, and make it safe enough to enter when it arrives.

That is, I think, what experience design is for. Not to produce insight — insight is cheap. To produce the kind of contact with reality that makes forgetting slightly harder.

Group dynamics · 2024

What the room already knows

Most of what goes unspoken in a meeting is already known by everyone in the room. The work is not to discover it. The work is to make it safe enough to say.

I have been in enough rooms to know that groups are remarkably good at knowing what they know and not saying it. The group senses the tension. It senses the person who is performing agreement. It senses the decision that has already been made by two people before the meeting started. It knows all of this and continues as if it does not.

This is not dishonesty, exactly. It is a form of collective self-protection. The unspoken thing, once spoken, cannot be unspoken. The room would have to reorganise around it. That reorganisation is uncertain. So the group does what groups often do — it manages the tension rather than resolving it, indefinitely, until it can no longer.

The facilitator's most useful question is rarely a question. It is a kind of witness: to name, neutrally and without drama, what appears to be present. Not to interpret. Not to solve. To make visible what the room was already seeing and not saying.

When a group becomes silent, something has been recognised
but not yet made speakable.

The skill is in the timing. Name the thing too early, and the room does not recognise it yet — it feels like an intrusion. Name it too late, and the group has already moved on to managing the discomfort, which is harder to interrupt.

What I have learned — slowly, from many rooms — is that the room itself usually knows when. There is a quality of attention that shifts just before something becomes nameable. The practised eye can see it. The practised silence can hold it.
